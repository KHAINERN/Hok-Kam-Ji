Lesson 1: Greetings and Basic Phrases

Common Greetings

Hokkien Kelantan Bahasa Malaysia
Li ho bo? Apa khabar?
Ho, wa ho Khabar baik
Chiak pa bo? Sudah makan?
Chiak pa Sudah makan
Bo Belum

Pronunciation Tips

  • "Li ho bo?" is pronounced as "lee ho bo?" with rising tone on "ho"
  • "Chiak" sounds like "ch-yak" with a short "a" sound
  • The "bo" at the end of questions is pronounced with a rising tone

Grammar Note

In Hokkien Kelantan, questions often end with "bo" (equivalent to "kah" in Bahasa Malaysia). This is a question particle that turns statements into yes/no questions.

Practice Dialogues

Dialogue 1:

Hokkien A: Li ho bo?

Hokkien B: Ho, wa ho. Li leh?

Malay A: Apa khabar?

Malay B: Khabar baik. Awak pula?

Common Verbs

Hokkien Kelantan Bahasa Malaysia
Ciak Makan
Lim Minum
Khun Tidur
Ki Pergi
Thak Baca

Pronunciation Tips

  • "Lim" is pronounced with a short "i" sound, similar to "lim" in Bahasa Malaysia but shorter
  • "Khun" has an aspirated "kh" sound at the beginning
  • "Ki" is pronounced with a high tone, similar to "key" in English but shorter

Grammar Note

Hokkien Kelantan verbs don't conjugate like Bahasa Malaysia verbs. The same form is used regardless of tense. Time is indicated by context or time words.

Hokkien Wa ciak (I eat/I ate/I will eat)
Malay Saya makan/sudah makan/akan makan

Practice Sentences

Malay Saya sudah makan.

Malay Kamu pergi ke mana?

Common Idioms

Hokkien Kelantan Literal Meaning Bahasa Malaysia Equivalent
Kha tui chiah chng Legs eat intestines Banyak sangat cakap
Chui tun tua tua Lips very big Suka bercakap besar
Bin ang ang Face red red Muka merah padam
Tham mo tua tua Beard very big Sombong

Pronunciation Tips

  • "Chiah" is pronounced with a falling tone
  • Reduplicated adjectives like "tua tua" maintain the same tone on both words
  • "Ang" (red) is pronounced with a high flat tone

Grammar Note

Hokkien Kelantan often uses reduplication for emphasis, similar to Bahasa Malaysia's "sangat" or "amat". Adjectives are often repeated for emphasis.

Hokkien Bin ang ang (Face very red)
Malay Muka sangat merah

Cultural Context

Many Hokkien Kelantan idioms come from physical characteristics or food. Understanding these cultural references helps in mastering the language.

Hokkien I kha tui chiah chng, bo si bo su.

Malay Dia banyak sangat cakap, bukan bukan pun dia cakap.

(He talks too much, even about unimportant things)

Hokkien Kelantan Grammar Guide

Sentence Structure

Hokkien Kelantan follows a Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) structure similar to Bahasa Malaysia:

Hokkien Wa ciak png (I eat rice)

Malay Saya makan nasi

Question Formation

Questions are often formed by adding question particles at the end:

Hokkien Ee ciak pa bo? (You eat already or not?)

Malay Awak sudah makan?

Negation

To negate verbs, "bo" is used before the verb:

Hokkien Wa bo ciak (I not eat)

Malay Saya tidak makan

Possession

Possession is indicated with "e" after the possessor:

Hokkien Wa e bo-yik (My book)

Malay Buku saya

Tense Markers

Hokkien Kelantan uses time words rather than verb conjugation:

Tense Hokkien Kelantan Bahasa Malaysia
Past Wa ciak pa (I eat already) Saya sudah makan
Present Wa ciak (I eat) Saya makan
Future Wa beh ciak (I will eat) Saya akan makan

Pronunciation Guide

Tone System

Hokkien Kelantan has a complex tone system with 5-7 tones depending on dialect. Key tones to master:

Tone Example Description
High level (55) si (poem) High flat pitch
Mid rising (35) sí (to try) Starts mid, rises sharply
Low falling (21) sì (time) Starts low, falls slightly
High falling (53) sî (city) Starts high, falls sharply

Common Sound Changes

Hokkien Kelantan has several unique sound features:

  • Final "-n" often becomes "-ng" (e.g., "bin" → "bing")
  • "S" at the beginning of words often becomes "h" (e.g., "suka" → "hukak")
  • Vowels are often nasalized (marked with "ⁿ" in linguistic notation)

Difficult Sounds

Aspirated Consonants

Hokkien has aspirated consonants that don't exist in Bahasa Malaysia:

  • kh - as in "khun" (sleep) - strong puff of air
  • th - as in "thak" (read) - aspirated "t"

Glottal Stops

Words ending with "-h" have a glottal stop (sudden cutoff):

  • "Bak" (meat) ends with a glottal stop
  • "Khoo" (to cry) has glottal constriction

Nasalized Vowels

Many vowels are nasalized in Hokkien Kelantan:

  • "Saa" (nasal voice) has strong nasalization
  • "Ea" (water) is pronounced with nasal quality
